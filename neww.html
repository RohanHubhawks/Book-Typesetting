श्रीमद्भगवदगीता जाना किधर है – भाग 1
In the minds of many people, there exists a
deep-rooted belief that the Mahabharata should
neither be read nor kept at home. However, in
the first chapter of the Mahabharata, a verse
declares:
ब्रह्मन् वेदरहस्यं च यच्चान्यत् स्थापितं मया ।
साङ्गोपनिषदां चैव वेदानां विस्तरक्रिया ॥
brahman vedarahasyaṁ ca yac cānyat
sthāpitaṁ mayā |
sāṅgopaniṣadāṁ caiva vedānāṁ vistara-
kriyā || 62 ||
When Sage Vyasa, through his divine vision,
perceived the entirety of this epic, he
addressed Lord Brahma, the creator of the
universe, saying that within this single
composition, he had gathered the hidden
essence of the Vedas, the wisdom of the
Upanishads along with all their branches, and
the substance of other sacred scriptures.
Indeed, this was no exaggeration. The
Mahabharata is a scripture that encompasses
everything.
It contains the essence of the Puranas, the
Vedas, and the Upanishads, along with its own

grand narrative. The sacred Bhagavad Gita
forms a part of it. The divine leelas (divine play)
of Krishna are described within it. The spirit and
summary of the Ramayana are also found in its
pages. It speaks of the origin of creation, of
medicine, charity, education, justice,
pilgrimage, oceans, death, disease, fear, old
age, and of the past, present, and future.
Understanding its profound meaning, this great
epic was written down by Ganesha as Vyasa
dictated it. Sage Vyasa composed it for the
welfare of sorrow-stricken humanity, so that
their suffering might be alleviated.
Further, it is said:
श्रद्दधानः सदा युक्तः सदा धर्मपरायणः ।
आसेवन्निममध्यायं नरः पापात् प्रमुच्यते ॥

āsevan imam adhyāyaṁ naraḥ pāpāt

In this verse, it is clearly stated that a person
who has faith, who remains devoted to

righteousness, and who regularly studies the
Gita’s chapters becomes freed from all sins.
Even when difficulties arise, such a person
remains untouched by sorrow. It is also
declared that one who recites it in the morning,
at noon, or in the evening attains freedom from
sin, and gets a long life, honor, and lasting
fame.
It is written that once the gods assembled and
placed the four Vedas on one side of a scale
and the Mahabharata on the other; the

kept at home and read daily. The Bhagavad

meant to be experienced. Only then does its

Yes, for a long time, this nectar of wisdom was

The Mahabharata in Brief
When Sage Vyasa Narrated the Epic
The Mahabharata is a blazing lamp in the
history of India. It is the greatest epic of Aryan
civilization and the eternal tradition of Hindu
culture. The great sage, the righteous Vyasa,
with his vision of knowledge, first perceived this
epic as though witnessing it directly, while
seated in a cave in the Himalayas, and then
resolved to compose it. For this purpose, he
sought counsel from Brahma, the creator of the

श्रीमद्भगवदगीता जाना किधर है – भाग 1
universe, asking: “How may I spread this most
revered epic throughout all the worlds? How
shall I teach it to my disciples? In this great
epic, I have elaborated not only the essence of
all the Vedas, but also their auxiliary disciplines
and the Upanishads.”
Thereupon, Brahma Himself appeared before
Vyasa and said, “Vyasa, since you have called
this work a poetic composition, it shall be
known as the first great poetic creation of this
universe. Therefore, your composition will be
renowned as an epic. No one in the world will
be able to create a work greater than this. To
have this epic written down, invoke Ganesha.”
Having said this, Brahma departed. Then, the
revered Sage Vyasa invoked Ganesha. When
Ganesha agreed to write, Vyasa began to
dictate complex and profound verses, for
Ganesha had laid down a condition: “If you are
unable to dictate the next verse immediately
after I finish writing the previous one, I will stop
writing.” Vyasa, in turn, placed his own
condition: “You must write each verse only after
fully understanding it.”

श्रीमद्भगवदगीता जाना किधर है – भाग 1
According to the Mahabharata tradition, Vyasa
composed 8,800 such verses whose meanings
were understood only by Vyasa, his son the
Sage Shukadeva, and Ganesha. At times,
when Ganesha paused for a moment to grasp
the deeper meaning of a verse, Vyasa would,
in that very interval, compose several more
verses in his mind. After attaining divine vision,
Sanjaya—the one who narrated the entire
Mahabharata to King Dhritarashtra—whether
he fully comprehended the meanings of these
8,800 cryptic verses or not is questioned in
verses 81–82 of the First Chapter of the
Mahabharata itself. These verses are woven
with such intricate depth that their mysteries
cannot be easily unraveled.

Background
In northern India, there was a region known as
the Kuru realm. In its royal palace lived a
valiant warrior named Bhishma, the protector of
the Kuru dynasty and its most senior member.
For the welfare of the kingdom, he had taken a
solemn vow never to marry. In time, this vow
also became one of the major causes of the
Mahabharata war. He was the eldest member
of the royal household. He was the son of King
Shantanu and Mother Ganga.
King Shantanu often went into the forest to
hunt fierce animals. One day, there he beheld a
woman of incomparable beauty. ‘साक्षात् श्रियम्
इव अपराम्’ (sākṣāt śriyam iva aparām) It
seemed as though she were Lakshmi herself
made manifest:

स कदाचिन्महाराज ददर्श परमां स्त्रियम् ।
जाज्वल्यमानां वपुषा साक्षाच्छ्रियमिवापराम् ॥ २७॥
sa kadācin mahārāja dadarśa paramāṁ
striyam |
jājvalyamānāṁ vapuṣā sākṣāc chriyam

(Lakshmi-like; it has also been stated this way in the Mahabharata
published by Gita Press.)

She too, upon seeing the king, became
spellbound. King Shantanu himself was radiant
and appeared like Indra, the king of the gods.
The king said to the woman, “Become my wife.”
The woman replied in her gentle voice, “I can
become your wife, but I have one condition:
Whatever I do, you must never restrain me. If
you ever do so, even by mistake, I shall leave
you.” King Shantanu accepted her condition.
Only then did he learn that the woman’s name
was Ganga.
When their first son was born, Ganga took the
child to the riverbank and immersed him in the
waters. King Shantanu had given his word that
he would not question her actions. Thereafter,
seven more sons were born, and Ganga let
each of them flow away into the river. When the
eighth son was born, the king could no longer
bear to see a child cast into the water.
Therefore, he questioned Ganga, asking why
she committed this grievous act of casting each
son into the river. Ganga said all eight of their
sons were actually the Vasus (divine beings).
They had been cursed by Sage Vashistha to
take birth on Earth and face many sufferings.
To save them from a life of pain and to give
them freedom (moksha), she, Goddess Ganga,
immersed them in the river right after their birth.
(However, King Shantanu stopped the
immersion of the eighth son, who would later
become famous as Bhishma Pitamah. Due to
the curse, he would be required to face many
struggles and sorrows throughout his life.)
Then, Ganga handed the eighth son to the king
and departed. This son was also called
Gangadatta.
Sometime later, King Shantanu married
Satyavati, and from this union a son named
Vichitravirya was born. The son of Shantanu
and Satyavati, Vichitravirya, later became the
father in the Kuru lineage from whom
Dhritarashtra, the father of the Kauravas,
descended.

श्रीमद्भगवदगीता जाना किधर है – भाग 1
The Reason Behind Bhishma’s Vow
When Bhishma was still young, his father, King
Shantanu, wished to marry a young woman
named Satyavati, who belonged to a lineage
considered lower than that of the king.
However, the Nishada chief, the foster-father of
the maiden, refused the marriage on the
grounds that even if a son were born to his
daughter, the son would not be made king,
since Bhishma, the elder son of King Shantanu,
already stood first in the line of succession.
Thereupon, Bhishma took a solemn vow before
his father that he would never marry and would
renounce all claim to the throne. In this way, he
removed himself from succession and sought
the maiden from the Nishada King for his
father. Thus, the father of the maiden
consented, and King Shantanu married
Satyavati. Later, it became known that
Satyavati was in truth the daughter of King
Vasu of Chedi, whom the Nishada chief had
merely raised and nurtured. Pleased with the
sacrifice of his son, King Shantanu granted
Bhishma a boon:
“Gangadatta shall attain death only by his own
will. He shall live as long as he desires.”
“Gangadatta shall possess the boon of icchā-
mṛtyu—the power to choose the time of his
own death.”
Due to this terrible and awe-inspiring vow
(Bhishma pratijñā), King Shantanu’s son came
to be known as Bhishma.
Yet, at that time, no one could have foreseen
that this very boon of death-at-will would one
day become one of the causes of the downfall
of the Kuru kingdom. Because of this boon,
Duryodhana later became so arrogant that he
began to regard Bhishma Pitamaha as
invincible and assumed victory in the war even
before it began. Had Bhishma not bound
himself to the Kuru throne in the name of duty
to the state, the war might not have taken
place. But the course of events is written by the
Divine long in advance; therefore, He is God,
for no one can fully comprehend what He has
ordained.

After some time, from the womb of Satyavati,
the first wise son, Chitrangada, was born.
Sometime later, her second son, Vichitravirya,
was born. While Vichitravirya was still young,
King Shantanu passed away. Thereafter,
Prince Gangadatta (Bhishma), according to the
wish of Mother Satyavati, placed Chitrangada
upon the throne.
Chitrangada lived in pride of his own valor. One
day, in battle with a Gandharva, he met his
death.

श्रीमद्भगवदगीता जाना किधर है – भाग 1
Bhishma’s First Great Mistake (The
Story of Gangadatta)
When Prince Vichitravirya came of age,
Bhishma Pitamaha went to the svayaṁvara
(the ceremony of choosing a groom) held at the
court of the king of Kashi and carried away the
three princesses of Kashi. Their names were
Amba, Ambika, and Ambalika. At that
svayaṁvara, Bhishma fought a fierce battle
with King Shalva. King Shalva was to be
married to the eldest princess, Amba.
The eldest princess came to Bhishma’s palace
and told him that, in her heart, she had already
chosen King Shalva as her husband. Upon
learning this, Bhishma granted permission to
the eldest princess, Amba, to return.
Thereafter, Bhishma arranged the marriage of
the remaining two princesses, Ambika and
Ambalika, with his younger half-brother
Vichitravirya.

श्रीमद्भगवदगीता जाना किधर है – भाग 1
Having taken Bhishma’s permission, Princess
Amba went to the city of King Shalva. However,
King Shalva told her, “You have already
belonged to another; therefore, I have no
desire to marry a woman like you. Return to
Bhishma, who carried you away.” But Amba did
not return to Bhishma, thinking, “I came away
from him with his own permission; with what
face shall I now return to Hastinapura (the
capital of the Kuru kingdom)?” Thinking thus,
she left the city. Yet, she resolved in her heart:
“The injustice done to me is only because of
Bhishma. I must take revenge upon him.”
Amba then went to a hermitage and narrated
her entire story to the ascetics residing there.
Present there was her grandfather, the great
ascetic Hotravahana, who accepted Amba as
his own daughter. After hearing everything, he
advised her to go and meet Parashurama, a
sage and warrior.
After meeting Parashurama, Amba told him her
entire story and prayed, “Please slay Bhishma.”
Thereupon, Parashurama came to Hastinapura
to fight Bhishma. When the two stood upon the
battlefield, Mother Ganga came there and
pleaded with Parashurama not to fight. Yet,
Bhishma did not withdraw and continued to
release arrows at Parashurama. For twenty-
three days, Bhishma and Parashurama fought
one another.

At last, Bhishma placed upon his bow an
extraordinary weapon called prasvāpanāstra.
Just as Bhishma was about to release this
prasvāpanāstra upon Parashurama, voices
arose from all directions, saying: “O Bhishma,
do not release this weapon upon Parashurama.
He is your guru and a knower of Brahman
(brahma-vettā), a great Brahmin.”
Hearing this, Bhishma withdrew the
prasvāpanāstra from his bow. Seeing this act of
restraint, Parashurama was pleased and said,
“This dull-witted one has been overcome by
Bhishma.” Thus, honoring the welfare of the
worlds, Bhishma and Parashurama withdrew
from the battle.
Seeing all this, Amba declared, “Now I shall go
to a place where I can attain the power to slay
Bhishma.” Saying this, she departed to perform
severe austerities. After many births, having
obtained a boon from Shiva, she was reborn as
Shikhandi and returned to fulfill her vow.

श्रीमद्भगवदगीता जाना किधर है – भाग 1
The Birth of Pandu, Dhritarashtra,
and Vidura
The younger half-brother of Bhishma and the
second son of King Shantanu, Vichitravirya,
died prematurely at a young age. As there was
no prince to inherit the throne, Mother Satyavati
urged Bhishma to beget sons from the two
queens. Bhishma refused, remaining faithful to
his vow.
Then Mother Satyavati approached the great
Sage Vyasa to obtain heirs for the kingdom
through the custom of niyoga—the ancient
practice by which a qualified sage is invited to
beget heirs for the continuation of a royal
lineage.
After the untimely death of Vichitravirya,
through the niyoga of Sage Vyasa,
Dhritarashtra was born from the womb of
Ambika. During the act of niyoga, Ambika, out
of fear, closed her eyes; therefore, though
Dhritarashtra was born, he was blind from birth.
Through the same niyoga, Pandu was born
from the womb of Ambalika. Seeing Vyasa,
Ambalika’s face had turned pale; therefore,
though she bore a son, he was afflicted with
weakness and jaundice from birth and was frail
by nature.
Along with this, one of the maidservants,
named Parashrava, was also sent to Sage
Vyasa for niyoga. She remained fearless and
humble; therefore, Vyasa blessed her, and
from her womb was born Vidura. Vidura was
highly learned and righteous; however, being
the son of a maidservant through niyoga, he
could not ascend the royal throne. Thus, three
sons were born in the lineage of Vichitravirya:
Dhritarashtra, Pandu, and Vidura. Vidura later
became a minister in the court of Dhritarashtra.

Because Bhishma was the great-grand-uncle
and guardian of the royal household, all people
addressed him with reverence as Pitāmaha.
“Revered grandsire; the elder patriarch of the
royal lineage.”
Bhishma Pitamaha raised Dhritarashtra,
Pandu, and the wise Vidura as his own sons.
He performed all their rites of passage and
oversaw their education. In a short time, all
three brothers became highly proficient in
archery (dhanurveda), mace-fighting
(gadāyuddha), and the use of shield and
sword. Bhishma Pitamaha also instructed them
in the Vedas, the Puranas, and all codes of
conduct and royal etiquette.
Upon the counsel of Vidura, Bhishma Pitamaha
arranged the marriage of Pandu with Princess
Pritha of the Yadava prince Shurasena. At that
time, in the entire world, there was no woman
equal to Pritha in beauty. Pritha had earlier
been adopted by King Kuntibhoja, as
Shurasena had vowed even before her birth to
give her to Kuntibhoja; therefore, she came to
be known as Kunti.

Along with this, Bhishma Pitamaha arranged
the marriage of Dhritarashtra with Gandhari,
the virtuous daughter of King Subala of
Gandhara. Sometime later, Bhishma Pitamaha
also arranged the marriage of Pandu with

Long before her marriage, Princess Pritha had
been entrusted by her father King Kuntibhoja
with the duty of serving guests who visited
their palace. One day, the Brahmin sage,
Durvasa, arrived at King Kuntibhoja’s house.
Sage Durvasa was of the highest spiritual
stature, but his nature was extremely stern and
his anger flared quickly; therefore, beings
throughout the universe feared him. Pritha served
him devotedly, as this duty had been assigned to
her. Pleased with her service, Sage
Durvasa—who could clearly perceive the future
of all—foreseeing the difficulties that would
befall Pritha, bestowed upon the princess a
mantra of invocation and explained the
complete vidhiḥ (method) for using it to uphold
righteousness. Finally, he said that by chanting
this mantra, whichever deity Pritha invoked
would grant her a son.
